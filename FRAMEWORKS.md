# Decision Frameworks Reference

Select 1–2 frameworks silently based on decision type. Apply them to sharpen the analysis — never name them to the user, never explain which one you're using.

---

## Framework Selection Guide

| Decision Type | Primary | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Professional (career, business, projects) | Pre-Mortem + Dependency Check | Reversibility Test |
| Personal (habits, skills, finances, life choices) | Reversibility Test + Regret Minimization | Status Quo Bias Check |
| Interpersonal (relationships, conflict, family) | Regret Minimization + Cost of Silence | Status Quo Bias Check |
| Mixed / Unclear | Reversibility Test | Status Quo Bias Check |

**When the decision type is ambiguous** (e.g., a business dispute with a family member, or a career move driven by relationship dynamics): identify which dimension carries more of the risk and weight the frameworks accordingly. A family business conflict is primarily interpersonal — use Cost of Silence as the anchor, not Pre-Mortem. A career decision influenced by a relationship is still primarily professional — use Pre-Mortem. When genuinely split, run both primary frameworks and let the stronger analysis win.

## The Frameworks

### 1. Pre-Mortem
**Best for:** Professional — business ideas, project launches, strategic bets

**What it does:** Assumes failure has already happened and forces backward reasoning. Not "could this fail?" but "it failed — what specifically went wrong?"

**Questions it forces:**
- What's the single most likely point of failure?
- Which assumption are you least willing to examine?
- What would you have wished you tested earlier?
- Who or what benefits from this failing? (inertia, competitor, sunk cost?)

**How to apply:** Use to generate the Against bullets. Each one should name a specific failure mechanism — not "market risk" but "the target customer already solves this with spreadsheets and won't pay to replace a free habit."

**Red flag:** If the failure modes feel obvious in hindsight, they were knowable in advance. Name them explicitly rather than softening them.

---

### 2. Reversibility Test
**Best for:** Any decision with real switching costs or long-term commitment

**What it does:** Separates one-way doors from two-way doors. Irreversible decisions deserve more deliberation. Reversible ones are being over-deliberated.

**Questions it forces:**
- If this goes badly, what does it actually cost to undo?
- Is the hesitation about real irreversibility or just discomfort with change?
- What's the concrete worst case — and is it survivable?
- Are you treating a two-way door like a one-way door?

**How to apply:** Use to calibrate verdict confidence and urgency of next actions. High reversibility → bias toward action. Low reversibility → one more cheap test before committing.

**Red flag:** If the person can't name what undoing this would cost them concretely, they're probably overestimating the stakes.

---

### 3. Regret Minimization
**Best for:** Personal and interpersonal decisions driven by fear or comfort-seeking

**What it does:** Shifts the time horizon to cut through short-term noise. At 80, which choice would you regret more — doing it or not doing it?

**Questions it forces:**
- Is the resistance genuine risk assessment or fear of short-term discomfort?
- Which failure is more recoverable: trying and failing, or never trying?
- What story will you tell about this moment in 10 years?

**How to apply:** Use when the Against case is mostly fear-based rather than structurally grounded. Separates real risk from noise. Often shifts verdict toward action for personal decisions where inertia is the real enemy.

**Red flag:** Early-career people almost always underweight the regret of inaction. If the Against case is mostly about discomfort, flag it — but don't use this framework to rationalize away legitimate structural risk.

---

### 4. Dependency Check
**Best for:** Business ideas, multi-part projects, strategies with moving pieces

**What it does:** Forces explicit mapping of what must be true for the plan to succeed. Each dependency is a potential failure point.

**Questions it forces:**
- What are the 3–5 things that must be true for this to work?
- Which can be tested cheaply before full commitment?
- Which are entirely outside your control?
- Does success require other people to change their behavior?

**How to apply:** Use to generate "What actually matters" bullets. The real levers are dependencies that are both high-impact and testable. Surface the assumptions the person is treating as given.

**Red flag:** If success requires other people to behave differently than they currently do — buy, adopt, change habits — that dependency is almost always underweighted.

---

### 5. Status Quo Bias Check
**Best for:** Any decision where inaction is the default option

**What it does:** Forces honest examination of whether staying put is rational or just comfortable. The status quo always has costs — they're invisible because they're already priced in.

**Questions it forces:**
- What is the real cost of NOT changing? (It's not zero)
- If starting fresh with no prior commitments, would you choose this situation?
- Are you defending the status quo because it's genuinely better, or because change is hard?
- What are you giving up by staying?

**How to apply:** Use when someone seems to be looking for permission to stay put rather than genuine analysis. Name the cost of inaction explicitly in the verdict or Against section if it's being underweighted.

**Red flag:** If all the Against bullets are about the new option and none address what happens if they don't act, the analysis is one-sided. The status quo has failure modes too — name them.

---

### 6. Cost of Silence
**Best for:** Interpersonal decisions — whether to say something, confront, express feelings, address conflict

**What it does:** Forces accounting of what not acting costs over time. Most people weigh the risk of speaking up but not the compounding cost of staying quiet.

**Questions it forces:**
- How long have you been carrying this?
- What has not saying it already cost — energy, authenticity, how you show up?
- If you stay quiet for another year, what changes?
- Is the relationship already being affected by the unsaid thing?

**How to apply:** Use to fill in the risks of not speaking — which are usually larger and slower-burning than the risks of speaking. The "Why it might hurt" section naturally covers the risk of action; this framework surfaces the risk of inaction.

**Red flag:** If someone has been sitting on something for months or years, the cost of silence is already significant and compounding. Name it explicitly — don't let the analysis treat silence as the safe default.

## Anti-Patterns

These combinations produce bad analysis. Avoid them:

- **Regret Minimization for professional decisions with real financial stakes.** "You'll regret not trying" is not analysis of a salary negotiation or business investment. It papers over legitimate risk with sentiment.
- **Pre-Mortem for interpersonal decisions.** "Imagine the relationship already failed" is clinical and lands wrong. Use Cost of Silence instead.
- **Dependency Check for personal habits.** "List your assumptions about going to the gym" produces nothing useful.
- **Cost of Silence for professional decisions.** It's designed for interpersonal dynamics, not strategy.
- **Regret Minimization to rationalize around genuine structural risk.** If the Against case contains real, named failure mechanisms (not just discomfort), this framework doesn't override them — it only applies when fear rather than structure is driving the Against case.

## Output Quality Checks

Run all four before writing the final output. Rewrite any bullet that fails.

### 1. Specificity Test
Could this bullet apply to literally any decision in this category? If yes — it's generic. Rewrite it.

- Generic: "The market is competitive"
- Specific: "You have no distribution channel and your only acquisition plan is organic social, which takes 12–18 months to produce signal you can act on"

### 2. Actionability Test
Can the person literally start the "What to do next" action today or this week?

- Generic: "Validate your idea"
- Specific: "Post a waitlist page with a single sentence description in one relevant community — if 10 people sign up in 48 hours unprompted, the demand signal is real"

### 3. Verdict Integrity Test
Does the verdict follow from the analysis, or was it softened at the end?

- If Against is stronger → verdict must lean No or Unclear
- If For is stronger → verdict must lean Yes
- "Yes, but be careful" after a strong Against case is evasion — rewrite the verdict
- "No, unless..." after a strong For case is the same failure in reverse — fix it

### 4. Generic Advice Test
Does "What to do next" contain any of the following? If yes, rewrite the bullet entirely:

- "Do more research"
- "Talk to someone you trust"
- "Think carefully about"
- "Consider whether"
- "Make sure you"
- "Explore your options"
- "Weigh your options"
- "Reflect on what matters to you"
